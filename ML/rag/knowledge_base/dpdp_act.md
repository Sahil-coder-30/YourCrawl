# Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (India)

## Chapter I — Preliminary

### Section 1 — Short Title, Extent and Commencement
This Act may be called the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023. It extends to the whole of India and applies to the processing of digital personal data within the territory of India, whether collected online or offline and subsequently digitised. It also applies to processing outside India if it involves offering goods or services to Data Principals within India.

### Section 2 — Definitions

**Data Principal**: The individual to whom the personal data relates. In the case of a child, it refers to the parent or lawful guardian.

**Data Fiduciary**: Any person who alone or in conjunction with other persons determines the purpose and means of processing of personal data.

**Data Processor**: Any person who processes personal data on behalf of a Data Fiduciary.

**Consent Manager**: A person registered with the Board who acts as a single point of contact to enable a Data Principal to give, manage, review, and withdraw her consent through an accessible, transparent, and interoperable platform.

**Personal Data**: Any data about an individual who is identifiable by or in relation to such data.

**Processing**: Wholly or partly automated operation or set of operations performed on digital personal data, including collection, recording, organisation, structuring, storage, adaptation, retrieval, use, alignment, combination, indexing, sharing, disclosure by transmission, dissemination, restriction, erasure, or destruction.

**Significant Data Fiduciary**: A Data Fiduciary or class of Data Fiduciaries as may be notified by the Central Government based on assessment of relevant factors such as volume and sensitivity of data processed, risk to rights of Data Principal, potential impact on sovereignty, integrity, and security of India, risk to electoral democracy, security of the State, and public order.

## Chapter II — Obligations of Data Fiduciary

### Section 4 — Grounds for Processing Personal Data
A person may process the personal data of a Data Principal only in accordance with the provisions of this Act and for a lawful purpose:
(a) for which the Data Principal has given her consent; or
(b) for certain legitimate uses.

### Section 5 — Notice
Before seeking consent or as soon as reasonably practicable, a Data Fiduciary shall give notice to the Data Principal containing:
- A description of personal data to be processed and the purpose
- The manner of exercising rights under the Act
- The manner to make a complaint to the Board

### Section 6 — Consent
(1) Consent shall be free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous with a clear affirmative action.
(2) Consent shall be limited to personal data necessary for the specified purpose.
(3) Consent may be given through a Consent Manager.
(4) Every request for consent shall be accompanied by a notice.
(5) The Data Principal may withdraw consent at any time, with the ease of withdrawal being comparable to the ease of giving consent.
(6) Withdrawal of consent shall not affect the lawfulness of processing before withdrawal.

### Section 7 — Certain Legitimate Uses
Personal data may be processed without consent for:
- Performance of any function by the State, provision of subsidy, benefit, service, certificate, licence, or permit
- Fulfillment of any obligation under any law
- Compliance with judgments or orders
- Medical emergencies or threats to life
- Employment-related purposes
- Public interest (epidemic response, disaster management)

### Section 8 — General Obligations of Data Fiduciary
(1) Ensure completeness, accuracy and consistency of personal data
(2) Implement appropriate technical and organisational measures for compliance
(3) Protect personal data by taking reasonable security safeguards
(4) In case of personal data breach, notify the Board and affected Data Principals
(5) Erase personal data no longer needed for the purpose unless retention required by law
(6) Publish business contact information of a Data Protection Officer or designated person
(7) Establish an effective grievance redressal mechanism

### Section 9 — Obligations of Significant Data Fiduciary
- Appoint a Data Protection Officer (resident of India)
- Appoint an independent data auditor for periodic audits
- Conduct Data Protection Impact Assessments
- Undertake periodic audit of policies and conduct

### Section 10 — Processing of Personal Data of Children
(1) Before processing personal data of a child or person with disability, obtain verifiable consent from parent or lawful guardian.
(2) Shall not undertake tracking or behavioural monitoring of children or targeted advertising directed at children.
(3) Shall not process personal data in any manner detrimental to the well-being of a child.
(4) The Central Government may exempt certain Data Fiduciaries from these requirements if processing is verifiably safe.

## Chapter III — Rights and Duties of Data Principal

### Section 11 — Right to Access Information
Data Principal has the right to obtain a summary of personal data being processed, the processing activities, identities of all Data Fiduciaries and Data Processors with whom data has been shared, and any other information as prescribed.

### Section 12 — Right to Correction and Erasure
Data Principal has the right to correct, complete, update, or erase personal data for the purpose for which it was processed.

### Section 13 — Right of Grievance Redressal
Data Principal has the right to have grievances addressed by the Data Fiduciary, including a response within a prescribed time.

### Section 14 — Right to Nominate
Data Principal has the right to nominate another individual who can exercise rights on behalf of the Data Principal in case of death or incapacity.

### Section 15 — Duties of Data Principal
- Comply with applicable laws when exercising rights
- Not register a false or frivolous complaint
- Furnish only verifiably authentic information
- Not impersonate another person

## Chapter IV — Special Provisions

### Section 16 — Central Government Powers
The Central Government may exempt certain entities or categories of personal data from provisions based on State security, sovereignty, and public order considerations.

### Section 17 — Data Protection Board of India
Establishment of the Board with powers to:
- Determine non-compliance and impose penalties
- Direct remedial measures
- Accept voluntary undertakings

## Chapter V — Penalties

### Section 33 — Penalties
- Failure to take reasonable security safeguards: Up to ₹250 crore
- Failure to notify data breach: Up to ₹200 crore
- Non-fulfillment of obligations for children's data: Up to ₹200 crore
- Non-fulfillment of additional obligations by Significant Data Fiduciary: Up to ₹150 crore
- Breach of any other provision: Up to ₹50 crore
- Breach by Data Principal of duties: Up to ₹10,000

## Key Dark Pattern Implications

### Pre-Selected Checkboxes
Violates Section 6(1) — consent must be free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous with a clear affirmative action. Pre-selected checkboxes do not constitute valid consent.

### Hidden Information / Buried Terms
Violates Section 5 — notice must be given in clear and plain language. Burying important information violates the informed consent requirement.

### Hard to Cancel / Roach Motel
Violates Section 6(5) — ease of withdrawal of consent must be comparable to ease of giving consent. Making cancellation harder than sign-up is a direct violation.

### Forced Consent / Forced Action
Violates Section 6(1) and Section 7 — consent must be unconditional. Forcing users to consent to unnecessary processing as a condition for service access violates purpose limitation.

### Dark Patterns Targeting Children
Violates Section 10 — tracking, behavioural monitoring, and targeted advertising directed at children are prohibited. Manipulative patterns targeting children carry severe penalties.

### Confirm Shaming
Violates the spirit of Section 6(1) — using guilt or shame to influence consent undermines the "free" requirement. Consent obtained through emotional manipulation is not freely given.
